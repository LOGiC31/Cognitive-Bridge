Abstract
This project proposes "Cognitive Bridge," a privacy-first Chrome extension that simplifies complex medical jargon into layperson terms in real-time. By utilizing local LLM inference via Transformers.js, we aim to bridge the read- ability gap in healthcare while ensuring strict data privacy for sensitive patient information.
1 Introduction
Digital healthcare portals like MyChart provide patients with direct access to clinical notes, yet a significant "readability gap" persists. Medical documentation often requires reading skills above a 10th-grade level, while the average adult reads at an 8th-grade level. This linguistic barrier is a social determinant of health; misunderstanding di- agnoses often leads to treatment non-adherence and increased patient anxiety. Our "Cognitive Bridge" addresses this by building a local-first Chrome ex- tension that acts as a real-time translator, focusing on Semantic Preservation to ensure critical clinical nuances are retained.
2 Related Work
2.1 Medical Text Simplification
Current research often utilizes sequence-to- sequence models like T5 (Raffel et al., 2020). How- ever, models may suffer from semantic drift, where critical clinical terms are over-simplified or omit- ted, losing diagnostic nuance (Vora et al., 2025). While some existing applications provide glossary tooltips, they often lack the context-aware rewriting needed for patient comprehension.
2.2 Privacy-First & Local Inference
Most state-of-the-art NLP solutions rely on cloud- based APIs, posing risks for Personal Health Infor- mation (PHI). Recent advances in WebAssembly and Transformers.js have enabled local-first infer- ence (Xenova, 2023). "Cognitive Bridge" ensures

all PHI remains on the client-side, making zero net- work requests for inference to guarantee privacy.
3 Designs
Our architecture Figure 1 ensures no text is sent to external servers. To meet Manifest V3 constraints, we run NER and simplification locally via Trans- formers.js.
3.1 Technical Pipeline & Safety
The system uses a MutationObserver to detect medical context. We deploy a DistilBERT model fine-tuned on the BC5CDR dataset for NER (Li et al., 2016). To ensure safety, we adopt a pol- icy where full-sentence simplification is triggered only when the model's confidence-computed from the NER entity scores and the simplification model's sequence log-probability-exceeds a spe- cific threshold. When confidence is low, the system defaults to showing local retrieval-based tooltips from MedlinePlus to prevent hallucinations.
3.2 Optimization & Deployment
Models are converted to ONNX and optimized via 8-bit quantization, targeting a reduced foot- print (from ~200MB to ~50MB) for faster load- ing. Tooltips are injected via a Shadow DOM to avoid CSS style conflicts with the host patient portal. This setup ensures zero network requests during inference, and we will report client-side latency in milliseconds.

4.2 Evaluation & Baselines
We compare our system against two baselines: (1) glossary-only (no rewrite) and (2) an off-the-shelf T5 model. Metrics include the Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level, SARI score (Xu et al., 2016), and an Entity Preservation Rate (EPR) to verify that clinical entities are correctly explained or retained.
